1. Data Organization & Frequency Tables

Raw data is like a messy room. You can't find what you need until you organize it. Frequency tables are our primary tool for tidying up categorical data.

Definition: A Frequency Table lists items (categories) together with the number of times (frequency) they occur.

3. Relative Frequency

Comparing raw numbers can be misleading if sample sizes differ. Relative frequency levels the playing field by converting counts into proportions or percentages.

Formula:

Relative Frequency = Frequency / Total Number of Observations

Percentage = Relative Frequency Ã— 100%

Comparison Scenario

Which Sales Team is Better?

Team A

Sales Made: 80

Calls Made: 100

Success Rate: 80/100 = 80%

Team B

Sales Made: 160

Calls Made: 200

Success Rate: 160/200 = 80%

Insight: Although Team B made double the sales, their Relative Frequency (Success Rate) is identical to Team A. They are equally effective!

4. Histograms & Grouped Data

When dealing with continuous data (like age, height, time) or large ranges, we group data into "bins" or intervals. A Histogram visualizes this grouped data.

Key Concepts

  • Bins/Intervals: The ranges we group data into (e.g., 0-10, 10-20).
  • Class Width: The size of each interval.
  • Continuity: Unlike bar graphs, histogram bars touch each other to show continuous data.

Histogram vs Bar Graph

Feature Bar Graph Histogram
Data Type Categorical (Names) Quantitative (Numbers)
Bars Gaps between bars No gaps (touching)
X-Axis Categories Number Ranges

5. Cumulative Frequency

Sometimes we want to know "how many are below X?". Cumulative frequency is the running total of frequencies.

Real World Application

Product Life Testing (Light Bulbs)

We tested 100 light bulbs to see how long they last.

Interpretation: The curve shows that by 3000 hours, 60 bulbs (60%) had failed. This helps in setting warranty periods (e.g., setting a warranty at 1000 hours means only ~10% returns).
